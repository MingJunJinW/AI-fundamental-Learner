Artificial Intelligence (AI)

Definition of AI:

  • Perception: AI can sense and interpret its environment.
  • Reasoning: It can analyze and draw conclusions.
  • Learning: It improves through experience (ML).
  • Action: It takes steps toward goals based on input.

What is Artificial Intelligence(AI)?

Back in the 1950s, legendary mathematician Alan Turing — a founder of the computer science — posed a groundbreaking question: "Can machines think?"

This question sparked a revolution. And soon after, AI emerged as an exciting new scientific field.


AI grew as a specialized field within computer science, the discipline that powers everything from hardware and programming languages to operating systems and algorithms.

Computer science is a vast field with many interconnected areas, and AI became one of its most fascinating branches.

In the 1950s, John McCarthy — a founder of the computer science — described it as the science and engineering of making intelligent machines. Over time, this definition expanded to describe AI as the creation of machines that learn, reason, making decisions, and mimic human-like itellignce to solve problems.

In today's data-driven world, the European Commission defines AI as systems that perceive their environment, collect and interpret data, reason over inferred knowledge, make decisions, and take actions to achieve a goal.

AGI vs ANI: Two Types of AI

Artificial Intelligence can be classified into two broad types: ANI and AGI. Most AI you see today is ANI (Artificial Narrow Intelligence), built for specific tasks. AGI (Artificial General Intelligence) refers to AI that can understand, learn, and perform any intellectual task that a human can.


ANI (Artificial Narrow Intelligence)

  • Designed for a specific task
  • Highly efficient in a narrow domain
  • Examples: Chatbots, voice assistants, spam filters
  • Cannot generalize beyond its training

AGI (Artificial General Intelligence)

  • Can learn and perform any intellectual task
  • Has human-like or exceed reasoning and adaptability
  • Still theoretical — not yet achieved
  • Goal: replicate general human intelligence

Comparison: ANI vs AGI

Artificial Narrow Intelligence (ANI)

Performs a single task extremely well, such as language translation, facial recognition, or recommendation engines. Most AI we see today is ANI.

Artificial General Intelligence (AGI)

Capable of understanding, learning, and applying intelligence across a broad range of tasks, similar to human cognition. Still theoretical and not yet realized.

Predictive vs. Generative AI

Predictive AI and Generative AI are two of the various broad classes of AI, each of which has a different range of uses.

  • Predictive AI is used to make predictions about the future based on historical data or in inference tasks like classiying data examples based on previously classified(labeled) examples.
  • Meanwhile, Generative AI is being widely used recently for creating new content, namely text, images, and audio generation, based on the properties and patterns of existing content.


Predictive AI

  • It is commonly used in areas such as credit scoring, stock price forecasting, spam filtering, and medical diagnoses.

Generative AI

  • Tools like ChatGPT, DALL·E, and MusicLM are examples of generative models that can produce essays, artwork, or even music from simple prompts.

Together, they complement each other — Predictive AI supports decision-making, while Generative AI fuels creativity and content generation.

Algorithms in Computer Science vs. AI Algorithms:

Classical Algorithms

Traditional algorithms follow a strict set of well-defined instructions. They solve problems by applying fixed, logical steps.

Examples: Calculating the cost, sorting numbers, searching in databases, finding the shortest path in a network (Dijkstra's algorithm).

AI Algorithms

AI algorithms learn patterns from data. They adjust themselves over time, rather than following only pre-set instructions.

Examples: Machine Learning models recommending model, predicting house prices, Neural Networks recognizing faces, Reinforcement Learning for self-driving cars.

AI Algorithms:

Learn by themselves to produce better outputs or processes from input data.

Have ability to learn to produce better outputs or to improve their processes by themselves, adapting to the input data; whereas convertional computing algorithms have a more rigid and deterministic input-output behavior.

By contrast, an AI system encompasses the broader infrastructure and components needed to implement and deploy our AI algorithm into the real world.
This includes elements like hardware and software, database and data storage, network infrastructure, and so on...

Acquiring Data:

"Data" is the fuel that makes AI work. Different ways AI systems can acquire data.

Depending on the type of AI system, there are different approaches to data acquisition:

  • Sensing the Environment:

    • Collect outside sensory information through sensors: mimic human sensors.
    • Transform perceptions into data.
    • The perception of smell or taste by AI is still not too explored, but the rest of the senses are already commonly used.

    • Occurs in:
      • NLP and audio: captruing speech, sounds
      • Computer Vision: satellite images, fingerprint, etc.
      • Robotics and sensors: temperature, touch, motion, gravity, etc. (e.g. Internet of Things(IT))

  • Datasets:

    • In general, AI systems deal with sets of input data called: datasets.
      • Dataset collection of data: data samples or instances of a given type of data

    • Structured: tabular format, spreadsheets (e.g. Excel files)
    • Unstructured: images, audio, videos, text, ...etc.

Learn & reason from data:

Once data has been acquired, almost every AI system nowadays relies on Machine Learning and Deep Learning techniques.

  • Machine Learning: learn from data and identify patterns.
    • Supervised Learning: for predictive tasks
      • classification: assign each data observation the category(class) it may belong to.
        • Binary classification: two classes, e.g. postitive/negative, male/female, etc.
        • Multi-class classification: several mutually exclusive class, e.g. multiple species
      • regression and forecasting
        • Regression: assign each data observation a numerical output or label based on its inputs.
        • Time series forecasting: predict future values of variable, base on its past behavior

    • Unsupervised Learning: specializes in other pattern recognition tasks
      • Clustering: find subgroups oof data with similar characteristics (e.g. K-means algorithm)
      • Anomaly detection: detecting abnormal data observations (e.g. unusual card transactions)
      • Association rule discovery: find common co-occurrences of items in transaction data (e.g. a popular problem in retail to find items that are frequently bought together)

    • Reinforcement Learning: investigates agents that learn by themselves from past experiences to master a complex task (e.g., like navigating a maze or playing videogrames by trial and error)

  • Deep Learning: use so-called neural networks to solve much more complex variants of all of those ML problems.
    • Highly sophisciated models based on deep neural networkL solve very challenging tasks where classical ML models become limited.
    • Learn form data as a human brain would do.
      • Deep learning models look for patterns in pictures, text, sounds, and other data by using so-called deep neural networks.
      • There powerful models require huge amounts of data to be trained.


Interacting with the Environment:

Interacting with the physical or digital environment to some degree.

  • Computer Vision:
    • Image processing: intelligently enhance images and video
    • Object detection: identify subjects in images/videos for surveillance, logistics, etc.
    • Motion analysis: extract motion information like speed and direction of objects
    • Image and video generation: create realistic visual data from human text

  • Natural Language Processing(NLP):
    • Text-based:
      • Text classification
      • Sentiment analysis: extract positive and negative feelings in text, e.g., customer reviews
      • Question answering(e.g. chatbots)
      • Text summarization
    • Speech-based:
      • Text-to-speech
      • Speech-to-text

  • Robotics:
    • Combine computer vision and NLP
      • Robots combine computer vision and NLP in a wide number of applications.

      • Usually, robots can be divided into the following areas:
        • Sensing and perception: collecting data or perceiving signals
        • Mobility: moving in the environment guided by perceptions of surroundings
        • Manipulation: the robot modifies its environment
        • Huamn-robot interaction(humanoid robots): e.g. converational robots endowed with NLP
